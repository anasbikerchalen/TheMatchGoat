The Ultimate Guide to Moneyline Betting: Odds, Strategy & How to Win

Navigating the world of sports betting can feel overwhelming, with a unique language of numbers, signs, and terms. Among the most fundamental and popular wagers is the moneyline bet. While simple at its core, understanding the nuances of moneyline odds is the first step toward making smarter, more informed betting decisions.

This comprehensive guide will break down everything you need to know. We'll explain how to read moneyline odds, calculate your potential winnings, and explore key strategies to increase your profitability.

What is a Moneyline Bet in Simple Terms?

A moneyline bet is the most straightforward wager in sports: you are simply betting on which team or athlete will win the contest. There are no point spreads to cover or conditions to meet. If the side you bet on wins, you win your bet. It's that simple.

How to Read Moneyline Odds: Favorites vs. Underdogs

In American odds, the standard format used by most U.S. sportsbooks, odds are displayed with a plus (+) or minus (-) sign, indicating the underdog and the favorite.

  • The Favorite (-): The team expected to win is the favorite and will have a minus (-) sign. This number shows how much you must bet to win $100. For example, if a team is -150, you need to risk $150 to win $100.
  • The Underdog (+): The team less likely to win is the underdog, marked with a plus (+) sign. This number shows how much profit you will win for every $100 you bet. For example, if a team is +150, a $100 bet would win you $150.

Decoding Positive (+) Moneyline Odds: Real Examples

Positive odds, or "plus money," indicate how much you'll profit from a $100 wager. Let's look at some common examples:

What does a +120 moneyline mean?

A +120 moneyline signifies the underdog. A successful $100 bet on these odds will return a profit of $120.

What is a +150 moneyline?

Similarly, at +150 odds, a winning $100 wager will earn you a $150 profit.

What does +130 moneyline mean?

A +130 moneyline means a $100 bet would win you $130. You are getting a better payout because this team is a slightly bigger underdog than one at +120.

What does +200 mean for odds?

Odds of +200 offer a 2-to-1 return on your investment. A winning $100 bet nets a $200 profit.

What do +500 odds to win mean?

This indicates a significant underdog. While riskier, the reward is substantial: a winning $100 bet would result in a $500 profit.

How to Calculate Your Payouts

You don't have to bet $100. That number is just the standard for understanding the odds. Here’s how to calculate your potential profit for any amount:

For Underdog (Positive +) Odds:

Formula: (Stake x (Odds / 100)) = Profit

Example (How much do you win on a +120 bet?): If you bet $20 on +120 odds, your calculation is: ($20 x (120 / 100)) = $24 profit. Your total payout would be $44 ($20 stake + $24 profit).

For Favorite (Negative -) Odds:

Formula: (Stake / (Odds / 100)) = Profit

Example: If you bet $90 on -150 odds, your calculation is: ($90 / (150 / 100)) = $60 profit. Your total payout would be $150 ($90 stake + $60 profit).

Winning Moneyline Betting Strategy

Understanding the odds is only half the battle. A successful long-term approach requires strategy.

Is it Better to Bet on Favorites or Underdogs?

This is the central debate in moneyline betting.

  • Betting Favorites: You will win more often, but your payouts will be smaller. The risk is that a single upset can wipe out the profit from several winning bets. Simply betting on every favorite is rarely a profitable long-term strategy.
  • Betting Underdogs: You will lose more bets than you win, but the higher payouts mean you only need to win a smaller percentage of your bets to be profitable. The key is identifying live underdogs—teams whose chances of winning are better than the odds imply.

Ultimately, the most profitable moneyline bets come from finding value, regardless of whether it's a favorite or an underdog.

What is the Best Moneyline Strategy?

A smart moneyline strategy is less about picking winners and more about identifying odds that are not accurate.

  • Do Your Homework: Go beyond the win-loss record. Analyze recent performance, head-to-head matchups, injuries, and situational factors like travel and rest.
  • Find Value: The goal is to determine if a team's actual probability of winning is higher than the implied probability of the odds. If you believe a +150 underdog has a 50% chance to win (the implied probability is only 40%), you have found a value bet.
  • Manage Your Bankroll: Never bet more than you can afford to lose. A disciplined approach to bankroll management will help you withstand losing streaks and stay in the game.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ) About Moneyline Betting

Q: Do moneyline favorites usually win?

A: Yes. Historically, moneyline favorites in major sports leagues win roughly two-thirds of the time. However, their low payouts mean you must win at a very high rate to be profitable.

Q: Is the moneyline the safest bet?

A: Because of its simplicity, it can feel safe. However, no bet is truly "safe." Betting on a heavy favorite at -500 offers a very high chance of winning but a tiny return, while a single upset results in a large loss. Risk and reward are always connected.

Q: What are the most profitable moneyline bets?

A: The most profitable wagers are value bets. These are often found on slight underdogs or even small favorites where the public perception might be skewed. Finding teams the oddsmakers have underestimated is the key to long-term profit.
